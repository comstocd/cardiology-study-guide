ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction (STEMI)

HIGH YIELD
üö® TIME IS MYOCARDIUM
  • Door-to-balloon time: ‚â§90 minutes (primary PCI)
  • Door-to-needle time: ‚â§30 minutes (if fibrinolysis chosen)
  • First medical contact to device: ‚â§120 minutes if transfer for PCI
üìà STEMI ECG CRITERIA
  • ST elevation: ‚â•1mm in ‚â•2 contiguous leads
  • Anterior: V1-V4 (LAD)
  • Inferior: II, III, aVF (RCA or LCx)
  • Lateral: I, aVL, V5-V6 (LCx or diagonal)
  • Posterior: ST depression V1-V3 (get V7-V9; LCx or RCA)
  • Right ventricular: V4R ST elevation with inferior STEMI (proximal RCA)
STEMI Equivalents
  • New LBBB: With clinical suspicion (use modified Sgarbossa criteria)
  • De Winter T waves: Upsloping ST depression V1-V6 + tall symmetric T waves = proximal LAD occlusion
  • Wellens' syndrome: Biphasic or deeply inverted T waves V2-V3 in pain-free patient = critical LAD stenosis (avoid stress test ‚Üí cath)
  • Posterior STEMI: Isolated ST depression V1-V3
  • Hyperacute T waves: Tall, symmetric, peaked T waves (earliest sign)
üìò ACC/AHA STEMI Guidelines (2013, focused update 2017)
Primary PCI is preferred reperfusion strategy when:
  • PCI-capable hospital (door-to-balloon ‚â§90 min)
  • Transfer possible with FMC-to-device ‚â§120 min
  • Contraindication to fibrinolysis
  • Cardiogenic shock
  • Diagnosis uncertain
Acute STEMI Medications
  • Aspirin: 162-325mg chewed immediately (continue 81mg daily indefinitely)
  • P2Y12 inhibitor: Ticagrelor 180mg or prasugrel 60mg (preferred for PCI); clopidogrel 600mg if lytic therapy or unable to take others
  • Anticoagulation: UFH, bivalirudin, or enoxaparin (per protocol)
  • High-intensity statin: Start in hospital (atorvastatin 80mg or rosuvastatin 40mg)
  • Beta-blocker: Within 24h if no contraindications (HF, low output, heart block, active bronchospasm)
  • ACEi/ARB: Within 24h, especially if anterior MI, HF, or EF ‚â§40%
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: DAPT Duration
  • Post-STEMI/ACS with stent: DAPT √ó 12 months (minimum)
  • High bleeding risk: May shorten to 6 months
  • Extended DAPT: Consider beyond 12 months if low bleeding risk & tolerated well
  • Prasugrel: Avoid if prior stroke/TIA, age ‚â•75, weight <60kg
üìù CLASSIC BOARD VIGNETTE: Right Ventricular MI

Scenario: Patient with inferior STEMI develops hypotension after nitroglycerin. Clear lung fields, JVD present.

Answer: Right ventricular infarction (proximal RCA). Avoid nitrates and diuretics. Treatment: IV fluids to maintain RV preload. Get V4R (ST elevation confirms RV involvement).

NSTEMI / Unstable Angina

HIGH YIELD
Definitions
  • NSTEMI: Elevated troponin + ischemic symptoms/ECG changes WITHOUT ST elevation
  • Unstable angina: Ischemic symptoms without troponin elevation (now rare with high-sensitivity troponin)
Risk Stratification: TIMI Risk Score
  • Age ‚â•65 (1 point)
  • ‚â•3 CAD risk factors (1 point)
  • Known CAD (‚â•50% stenosis) (1 point)
  • Aspirin use within 7 days (1 point)
  • Severe angina (‚â•2 episodes in 24h) (1 point)
  • ST deviation ‚â•0.5mm (1 point)
  • Positive cardiac biomarker (1 point)

0-2: Low risk; 3-4: Intermediate; 5-7: High risk (favor early invasive)

üìò ACC/AHA NSTE-ACS Guidelines (2014)
Invasive Strategy Timing:
  • Immediate (<2h): Refractory angina, hemodynamic/electrical instability, acute HF
  • Early (within 24h): GRACE score >140, troponin rise/fall, dynamic ST changes
  • Delayed invasive (25-72h): None of above but TIMI ‚â•2, diabetes, EF <40%, GFR <60, prior CABG
  • Ischemia-guided: Low-risk, prefer medical management (stress test, then cath if positive)
Medical Therapy
  • Aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor: Same as STEMI
  • Anticoagulation: UFH, enoxaparin, fondaparinux, or bivalirudin
  • Beta-blocker: Oral within 24h (IV if ongoing ischemia, HTN, tachycardia)
  • Nitrates: For ongoing ischemia (avoid if RV infarct, severe AS, recent PDE5 inhibitor)
  • Statin: High-intensity, started in hospital
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Troponin Interpretation
  • High-sensitivity troponin: Detects smaller infarcts, earlier rise (1-3h)
  • Rise and/or fall pattern: Suggests acute MI (not chronic elevation)
  • Non-ACS causes of troponin elevation: HF, PE, myocarditis, sepsis, renal failure, takotsubo, arrhythmias, cardiac contusion

üìù CLASSIC BOARD VIGNETTE: Post-MI Murmur

Scenario: 5 days post-MI, patient develops sudden hypotension and new harsh holosystolic murmur with thrill at left lower sternal border.

Answer: Ventricular septal rupture. Echo will show VSD with left-to-right shunt. Management: IABP to reduce afterload, stabilize, and emergent surgical repair.

‚ö° BOARD PEARL: VSD vs Papillary Muscle Rupture

  • Confirm with echo

Revascularization: PCI vs CABG

HIGH YIELD
üìò ACC/AHA/SCAI Revascularization Guidelines
CABG generally preferred over PCI for:
  • Left main disease (especially with SYNTAX score >22)
  • Three-vessel disease, especially with diabetes
  • Complex CAD with high SYNTAX score (>32)
  • Reduced EF (<35%)
PCI reasonable for:
  • 1-2 vessel disease without proximal LAD
  • Left main with low SYNTAX (‚â§22) if surgery high risk
  • Patient factors favoring less invasive approach
üíé SYNTAX SCORE
  • Angiographic scoring system assessing CAD complexity
  • Low (0-22): PCI outcomes similar to CABG
  • Intermediate (23-32): Heart team discussion
  • High (>32): CABG preferred (better outcomes)
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Diabetes and Revascularization (FREEDOM Trial)
  • FREEDOM (2012): Diabetics with multivessel CAD had better outcomes with CABG vs PCI
  • Lower death, MI, stroke at 5 years with CABG
  • Key: Use LIMA to LAD (survival benefit)

Rate vs Rhythm Control

HIGH YIELD
üìò ACC/AHA/HRS AF Guidelines (2023 Update)
Rate Control Target:
  • Lenient: Resting HR <110 bpm (RACE II trial ‚Äî noninferior)
  • Strict: Resting HR <80 bpm (consider if symptomatic)
Rate Control Agents:
  • Beta-blockers: First-line (metoprolol, atenolol, carvedilol)
  • Non-DHP CCB: Diltiazem, verapamil (avoid in HFrEF)
  • Digoxin: Add-on or if HF with hypotension
  • Amiodarone: If above fail; more for rhythm but slows rate
Rhythm Control Considerations
  • Preferred if: Symptomatic despite rate control, younger patients, first episode, tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy
  • EAST-AFNET 4 (2020): Early rhythm control (within 1 year of diagnosis) reduced CV outcomes vs rate control
  • Methods: Antiarrhythmic drugs, electrical cardioversion, catheter ablation

Supraventricular Tachycardia

HIGH YIELD
SVT Classification
  • AVNRT: Most common (60%); slow-fast pathway in AV node
  • AVRT: Accessory pathway (WPW if preexcitation on baseline ECG)
  • Atrial tachycardia: Focal or macroreentrant (atrial flutter)
Acute Management
  • Vagal maneuvers: Carotid massage, Valsalva (modified ‚Äî blow then lie flat with legs raised)
  • Adenosine: 6mg rapid IV push, then 12mg if needed (use central line if available)
  • If adenosine fails: Diltiazem IV, beta-blocker, or cardioversion
  • Unstable: Synchronized cardioversion
üö® WPW + AFIB
DO NOT give AV nodal blockers (adenosine, diltiazem, digoxin, verapamil)
  • Blocks AV node ‚Üí conduction via accessory pathway ‚Üí rapid ventricular response ‚Üí VF
  • Treatment: Procainamide or cardioversion
  • ECG clue: Irregularly irregular wide complex tachycardia with varying QRS morphology
üìà WPW ECG FEATURES (Sinus Rhythm)
  • Delta wave: Slurred upstroke of QRS
  • Short PR interval: <120ms
  • Wide QRS: Due to delta wave
  • Secondary ST-T changes

Ventricular Tachycardia

HIGH YIELD
VT vs SVT with Aberrancy

Assume VT until proven otherwise (especially if structural heart disease)

  • Favors VT: AV dissociation, fusion/capture beats, QRS >140ms, extreme axis deviation, precordial concordance
  • Favors SVT: Typical RBBB or LBBB pattern, preceding P waves
VT Management
  • Unstable: Synchronized cardioversion (100-200J biphasic)
  • Pulseless VT: Defibrillation + ACLS
  • Stable monomorphic VT: Amiodarone 150mg IV over 10 min; procainamide; cardioversion
  • Polymorphic VT (Torsades): IV magnesium 2g; correct QT-prolonging factors; isoproterenol/pacing if bradycardia-dependent
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Torsades de Pointes
  • Polymorphic VT + prolonged QT
  • Causes: Drugs (sotalol, amiodarone, antipsychotics, antibiotics), hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, bradycardia, congenital LQTS
  • Treatment: IV magnesium, correct electrolytes, stop offending drugs, overdrive pacing if refractory
üìù CLASSIC BOARD VIGNETTE

Scenario: Patient on sotalol develops syncope. ECG shows QTc 560ms with runs of polymorphic VT.

Answer: Drug-induced Torsades de Pointes. Stop sotalol, give IV magnesium 2g, correct K+ and Mg++, temporary pacing if needed.

Bradyarrhythmias

HIGH YIELD
AV Block Classification
  • 1st degree: PR >200ms, all P waves conducted
  • 2nd degree Mobitz I (Wenckebach): Progressive PR prolongation ‚Üí dropped beat; usually AV nodal
  • 2nd degree Mobitz II: Constant PR ‚Üí sudden dropped beat; infranodal, high risk of complete block
  • 3rd degree (complete): No relationship between P waves and QRS; requires pacing
üìò Pacemaker Indications
Class I (definite indications):
  • Symptomatic sinus node dysfunction
  • Third-degree AV block (regardless of symptoms)
  • Mobitz II second-degree AV block
  • Symptomatic chronotropic incompetence
  • Alternating bundle branch block
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Reversible Causes
Before permanent pacing, exclude reversible causes:
  • Drug-induced (beta-blockers, CCB, digoxin, amiodarone)
  • Lyme disease (especially if young, endemic area)
  • Electrolyte disturbances
  • Myocardial ischemia
  • Post-cardiac surgery (may resolve)

Secondary MR Management
  • First-line: GDMT for HF (may reduce MR severity)
  • CRT: If indicated for HF (may improve MR)
  • MitraClip: For select patients with HFrEF + severe MR despite GDMT (COAPT trial)
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: COAPT vs MITRA-FR
  • COAPT (positive): MitraClip reduced HF hospitalization/death in HFrEF with severe MR
  • MITRA-FR (negative): No benefit
  • Key difference: COAPT selected "disproportionately severe MR" (MR out of proportion to LV size)

Aortic Regurgitation & Mitral Stenosis

CORE
Aortic Regurgitation
  • Causes: Bicuspid valve, endocarditis, aortic root dilation, rheumatic
  • Physical exam: Wide pulse pressure, bounding pulses, diastolic decrescendo murmur at LUSB
  • Surgery indications:
    • Symptomatic severe AR
    • Asymptomatic with EF ‚â§55%
    • Asymptomatic with LVESD >50mm or LVEDD >65mm
Mitral Stenosis
  • Most common cause: Rheumatic heart disease
  • Symptoms: Dyspnea, orthopnea, hemoptysis, AF, systemic emboli
  • Physical exam: Opening snap, low-pitched diastolic rumble at apex
  • Severe MS: MVA <1.5 cm¬≤, mean gradient >10 mmHg
  • Intervention: PBMV (if favorable anatomy) or surgery; anticoagulation if AF
üö® MS + PREGNANCY
  • High-risk condition (increased blood volume ‚Üí pulmonary edema)
  • Beta-blockers for rate control
  • PBMV can be done during pregnancy if needed
  • Screen and treat before conception if possible

Prosthetic Valve Management

HIGH YIELD
Anticoagulation for Mechanical Valves
  • Aortic mechanical: INR 2.0-3.0 (+ low-dose aspirin)
  • Mitral mechanical: INR 2.5-3.5 (+ low-dose aspirin)
  • DOACs are CONTRAINDICATED for mechanical valves
Bioprosthetic Valves
  • Anticoagulation: Aspirin alone (some centers use warfarin √ó 3-6 months post-SAVR)
  • Post-TAVR: DAPT or aspirin + anticoagulant √ó 3-6 months, then aspirin alone
  • Durability: ~10-15 years (less in younger patients)
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Bridging Anticoagulation
  • High-risk (any mitral mechanical, older aortic mechanical, recent stroke/TIA): Bridge with heparin/LMWH
  • Low-risk (newer bileaflet aortic): May be able to hold briefly without bridging
  • Consult cardiology for periprocedural management

üíî Cardiomyopathies

ü´Ä

Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy

HIGH YIELD
Diagnosis & Features
  • Definition: LV wall thickness ‚â•15mm without loading condition explanation
  • Genetics: Autosomal dominant; sarcomere protein mutations
  • LVOT obstruction: Present in ~70% (dynamic; worsens with decreased preload/afterload)
  • Murmur: Harsh systolic crescendo-decrescendo at LLSB; increases with Valsalva/standing
Medical Management
  • Beta-blockers: First-line for symptoms
  • Non-DHP CCB (verapamil): If beta-blocker intolerant
  • Disopyramide: Add for refractory symptoms with obstruction
  • Mavacamten: Cardiac myosin inhibitor (EXPLORER-HCM) ‚Äî reduces LVOT gradient
  • Avoid: Vasodilators, diuretics, high-dose diuretics, digoxin
üö® SCD RISK STRATIFICATION IN HCM
ICD if any major risk factor:
  • Prior cardiac arrest or sustained VT
  • Family history of SCD from HCM
  • Maximum LV wall thickness ‚â•30mm
  • Unexplained syncope
  • NSVT on monitoring
  • LV apical aneurysm
  • EF <50%
Interventions for Obstruction
  • Septal reduction therapy: If drug-refractory symptoms + LVOT gradient ‚â•50 mmHg
  • Surgical myectomy: Gold standard; mortality <1% at experienced centers
  • Alcohol septal ablation: Alternative if high surgical risk or patient preference

Dilated, Restrictive & Other Cardiomyopathies

HIGH YIELD
Dilated Cardiomyopathy
  • Causes: Idiopathic, familial (30-50%), ischemic, viral, alcohol, peripartum, toxins (doxorubicin), tachycardia-mediated
  • Workup: Echo, coronary evaluation (angiography or CTA), consider MRI, genetic testing if familial
  • Treatment: GDMT for HFrEF; treat underlying cause
Restrictive Cardiomyopathy
  • Features: Impaired filling, normal/small ventricles, preserved EF initially
  • Causes: Amyloidosis (most common), hemochromatosis, sarcoidosis, radiation, endomyocardial fibrosis
  • Distinguish from constrictive pericarditis: Similar hemodynamics; MRI, CT, cath helpful
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Cardiac Amyloidosis
  • AL amyloidosis: Light chain (plasma cell dyscrasia); poor prognosis; treat underlying disease
  • ATTR (transthyretin): Wild-type (elderly) or hereditary; better prognosis
  • Tafamidis: TTR stabilizer; improves outcomes in ATTR-CM (ATTR-ACT trial)
  • Diagnosis: Technetium pyrophosphate scan (positive in ATTR, negative in AL); biopsy if needed
  • Avoid digoxin: Binds amyloid fibrils ‚Üí toxicity
Takotsubo (Stress) Cardiomyopathy
  • Trigger: Emotional or physical stress
  • Features: Apical ballooning, troponin elevation out of proportion to coronary disease
  • Angiography: Normal or non-obstructive coronaries
  • Prognosis: Usually resolves in weeks; supportive care
Arrhythmogenic RV Cardiomyopathy (ARVC)
  • Features: Fibrofatty replacement of RV; VT (often LBBB morphology)
  • ECG: Epsilon wave, T-wave inversions V1-V3
  • Diagnosis: Task Force criteria (MRI, echo, ECG, arrhythmias, family history)
  • Management: Avoid competitive sports; beta-blockers; ICD if high risk

üíì Pericardial Disease

ü´Ä

Acute Pericarditis

HIGH YIELD
Diagnosis (2 of 4 criteria)
  • Chest pain (sharp, pleuritic, positional ‚Äî better leaning forward)
  • Pericardial friction rub
  • ECG changes (diffuse ST elevation, PR depression)
  • New or worsening pericardial effusion
üìà PERICARDITIS ECG
  • Diffuse ST elevation (concave up, "smiley face")
  • PR depression (most specific finding)
  • No reciprocal changes (unlike STEMI)
  • Spodick sign: Downsloping TP segment
üìò Treatment (ESC 2015)
  • First-line: NSAIDs (ibuprofen 600mg TID or aspirin 750-1000mg TID) √ó 1-2 weeks + colchicine √ó 3 months
  • Colchicine: Reduces recurrence by 50% (COPE, ICAP trials)
  • Post-MI pericarditis: Aspirin preferred; avoid high-dose NSAIDs (impair healing)
  • Corticosteroids: AVOID if possible (increase recurrence); use only if refractory or contraindication to NSAIDs/colchicine
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: High-Risk Features
  • Fever >38¬∞C
  • Subacute onset
  • Large effusion or tamponade
  • Failure to respond to NSAIDs within 1 week
  • Immunocompromised
  • Trauma
  • Anticoagulation use
Consider hospitalization and further workup for underlying cause.

Cardiac Tamponade

HIGH YIELD
üö® CARDIAC TAMPONADE
Clinical triad (Beck's triad):
  • Hypotension
  • Jugular venous distension
  • Muffled heart sounds
Other findings: Pulsus paradoxus (>10 mmHg drop in SBP with inspiration), tachycardia
Echo Findings
  • RA systolic collapse
  • RV diastolic collapse (more specific)
  • IVC plethora (no respiratory variation)
  • Respiratory variation in mitral/tricuspid inflow
Management
  • Unstable: Emergent pericardiocentesis
  • Stable: Urgent drainage (echo-guided or surgical)
  • Volume resuscitation: May temporize while preparing for drainage
  • Avoid: Diuretics, positive pressure ventilation (reduce preload)
üìù CLASSIC BOARD VIGNETTE

Scenario: Post-cardiac surgery patient with hypotension, JVD, clear lungs, pulsus paradoxus. Echo shows moderate effusion with RV diastolic collapse.

Answer: Cardiac tamponade. Emergent pericardiocentesis or surgical drainage.

Constrictive Pericarditis

HIGH YIELD
Clinical Features
  • Right-sided HF symptoms (edema, ascites, JVD)
  • Kussmaul's sign: JVP increases (or fails to decrease) with inspiration
  • Pericardial knock: Early diastolic sound
Distinguishing from Restrictive Cardiomyopathy
Feature Constrictive Restrictive
Pericardial thickening Present Absent
Ventricular interdependence Present (septal bounce) Absent
Respiratory variation in E velocity ‚â•25% <15%
BNP Normal or mildly elevated Markedly elevated
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Treatment
  • Definitive: Pericardiectomy (surgical removal of pericardium)
  • Medical: Diuretics for congestion; anti-inflammatory therapy if active inflammation
  • Trial of anti-inflammatory: Consider in subacute/transient constriction (may resolve)

‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Resistant HTN

  • Rule out: Non-adherence, white coat HTN, secondary causes

Hypertensive Emergency

HIGH YIELD
üö® HYPERTENSIVE EMERGENCY
Definition: Severe HTN + acute end-organ damage (encephalopathy, AKI, pulmonary edema, aortic dissection, MI, stroke)
Treatment Principles
  • Goal: Reduce MAP by 20-25% in first hour (except aortic dissection ‚Äî faster)
  • IV agents: Nicardipine, labetalol, nitroprusside, clevidipine, esmolol
  • Aortic dissection: Beta-blocker FIRST (reduce dP/dt), then vasodilator; target SBP <120 and HR <60
  • Avoid: Rapid reduction (risk of ischemia)

Classification & Diagnosis

HIGH YIELD

  • Group 2: Left heart disease (most common)

  • Combined: mPAP >20, PCWP >15, PVR ‚â•3 WU

  • Done in Group 1 PAH only
  • Positive response: ‚ÜìmPAP ‚â•10 mmHg to ‚â§40 mmHg with normal/increased CO
  • ~10% are responders: May use high-dose CCB (only in responders!)

HIGH YIELD

ü©∫ Aortic Disease

Aortic Dissection

HIGH YIELD
üö® SURGICAL EMERGENCY (TYPE A)
Type A (Stanford): Involves ascending aorta ‚Üí surgery immediately
Type B: Descending only ‚Üí medical management unless complicated
Clinical Features
  • Classic presentation: Sudden, severe, "tearing" chest/back pain
  • Pulse deficits: Unequal arm BPs
  • Aortic regurgitation: If dissection extends to aortic root
  • Complications: MI (RCA involvement), stroke, limb ischemia, tamponade
Medical Management
  • Beta-blocker FIRST: Reduce heart rate (<60) and dP/dt before vasodilators
  • Target SBP: <120 mmHg
  • IV esmolol or labetalol
  • Add vasodilator if needed: Nicardipine, nitroprusside (only after beta-blocker)
‚ö° BOARD PEARL: Type B Complications Requiring Intervention
  • Malperfusion (renal, mesenteric, limb ischemia)
  • Rupture or impending rupture
  • Refractory pain or hypertension
  • Rapid expansion
Treatment: TEVAR (thoracic endovascular repair) preferred

Aortic Aneurysm

CORE
Thoracic Aortic Aneurysm (TAA)
  • Screening: First-degree relatives of patients with TAA or bicuspid aortic valve
  • Surgical threshold: ‚â•5.5 cm (‚â•5.0 in Marfan or bicuspid with risk factors)
  • Medical: BP control (beta-blocker preferred), statin, lifestyle
Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (AAA)
  • Screening: One-time ultrasound for men 65-75 who ever smoked
  • Repair threshold: ‚â•5.5 cm (or ‚â•5.0 in women), or rapid expansion (>0.5 cm/6 months)
  • Symptomatic: Urgent repair regardless of size
  • EVAR vs open: Similar long-term survival; EVAR lower perioperative mortality but more reinterventions

PAD Diagnosis & Management

HIGH YIELD
Ankle-Brachial Index (ABI)
  • Normal: 1.0-1.4
  • Borderline: 0.91-0.99
  • Abnormal (PAD): ‚â§0.90
  • Severe PAD: <0.40

ü©∏ Venous Thromboembolism

üíä

VTE Treatment

HIGH YIELD
üìò CHEST Guidelines / ASH 2020
Initial anticoagulation:
  • DOACs preferred over warfarin for most patients
  • Rivaroxaban or apixaban: Can start without parenteral lead-in
  • Dabigatran or edoxaban: Require 5-10 days parenteral first
  • Exceptions (use warfarin): Antiphospholipid syndrome, severe renal impairment

  • Provoked (surgery, immobilization): 3 months
  • Unprovoked: ‚â•3 months, then reassess for extended therapy

Pacemaker Modes & Indications

HIGH YIELD
Pacemaker Nomenclature (NBG Code)
  • 1st letter: Chamber paced (A, V, D=dual)
  • 2nd letter: Chamber sensed (A, V, D, O=none)
  • 3rd letter: Response to sensing (I=inhibit, T=trigger, D=both)

Mode Description Indications

  • Definition: Symptoms from loss of AV synchrony (VVI mode pacing with intact atrial contraction)

  • Treatment: Upgrade to dual-chamber (DDD) pacing

ICD & CRT Summary

HIGH YIELD
ICD Primary Prevention Indications
  • Ischemic CMP: EF ‚â§35%, NYHA II-III, ‚â•40 days post-MI, on GDMT
  • Non-ischemic CMP: EF ‚â§35%, NYHA II-III, ‚â•3 months GDMT
  • HCM: High-risk features (see HCM section)
  • Expected survival >1 year with good functional status
CRT Indications
  • EF ‚â§35% + NYHA II-IV
  • LBBB + QRS ‚â•150ms: Class I (strongest indication)
  • LBBB + QRS 120-149ms: Class IIa
  • Non-LBBB + QRS ‚â•150ms: Class IIa
  • QRS <120ms: No benefit

  • Lead dislodgement: Early post-implant; loss of capture/sensing
  • Pocket infection: Device removal required
  • Lead endocarditis: S. aureus most common; requires extraction
  • Inappropriate shocks: Common in ICDs; treat underlying cause (AF, lead fracture)
  • Twiddler syndrome: Patient manipulation of device causing lead displacement

Echocardiography

CORE

  • TEE preferred for: Endocarditis (vegetations, abscesses), LAA thrombus, prosthetic valve evaluation, aortic dissection, intracardiac shunts

HIGH YIELD

  • Infiltrative disease: Amyloid, sarcoid, hemochromatosis

  • Gadolinium: Avoid if eGFR <30 (nephrogenic systemic fibrosis risk)
  • MRI-conditional devices: May be scanned under specific conditions

Cardiac CT

CORE
Coronary CT Angiography (CCTA)
  • Excellent negative predictive value: Rules out significant CAD
  • Best for: Low-intermediate risk chest pain, acute chest pain in ED
